Marking life's
holiest moments.

From a child's first blessing to a marriage's first steps to a loved one's final rest — here's what to expect from each sacrament, and how to begin planning.

Baptism Chrismation Wedding Memorial Confession
Baptism

Welcoming a new member into the faith

Baptism is how a child — or an adult who wasn't baptized in infancy — is joined to Christ and the Church, through immersion in water and anointing with holy Chrism. It's usually followed by a celebration with family and godparents.

  • Godparents (sponsors): at least one godparent is required and must be a baptized, practicing Orthodox Christian in good standing with the Church.
  • Scheduling: baptisms are typically scheduled on Saturdays or after Sunday Liturgy. We recommend contacting the parish office at least 6–8 weeks in advance.
  • What's needed: a baptismal candle, a white garment (lambada), and a cross for the child — the parish office can point you to local suppliers if needed.
  • Adult baptism: adults coming to the faith for the first time typically go through a period of instruction with one of our priests beforehand.

Chrismation

Received into the Orthodox faith

Chrismation is the sacrament through which someone already baptized in the Trinitarian faith — most often a Christian coming to Orthodoxy from another tradition — is received into the Orthodox Church through anointing with holy Chrism.

  • Preparation: those preparing for Chrismation typically meet regularly with Fr. Rick or Fr. Peter, and many join our periodic Catechism course to go deeper into the faith first (see the Get Involved page).
  • Sponsor: a sponsor who is an Orthodox Christian in good standing is required, similar to a godparent at baptism.
  • Timing: Chrismation is often celebrated during a Divine Liturgy, so the person is welcomed into the parish in front of their new church family.

Wedding

Beginning your marriage in the Church

The Sacrament of Crowning unites a couple as a new household within the Church, asking God's blessing on their life together. It's one of the most joyful services in the Orthodox Church — full of ancient symbolism, from the crowns to the shared cup.

  • Scheduling: please reach out to the parish office at least 6 months before your intended date, and before booking a reception venue — some dates are restricted by the Church calendar (for example, during Great Lent).
  • Interfaith couples: we regularly celebrate weddings where one spouse is not Orthodox. A short conversation with one of our priests early on will help clarify what's needed for your specific situation.
  • Koumbaro/Koumbara (sponsor): at least one sponsor must be a baptized Orthodox Christian in good standing.
  • Pre-marital meeting: couples meet with the officiating priest ahead of time to prepare spiritually for the sacrament, not just the ceremony logistics.

Memorial & Funeral

Honoring and remembering loved ones

The Church walks with families through loss — from the funeral service itself through the memorial services (mnemosyna) that follow at set intervals, continuing to hold the departed and their family in prayer.

  • If a loved one has just passed: please call the parish office right away, any day of the week — (847) 729-2235, and dial 2 for after-hours emergencies. We will help you coordinate with a funeral home and set a service time.
  • Memorial services: traditionally offered 40 days, 6 months, and 1 year after a passing, and on the anniversary each year after. The parish office can help schedule these and prepare kollyva (memorial wheat).
  • 40-Day Blessing: this same rhythm of 40 days also marks a joyful occasion — the churching of a new mother and baby after birth.
Call the parish office
Confession

The sacrament of healing and renewal

Confession is a private, unhurried conversation with a priest — a chance to be honest before God about where you've fallen short, and to receive His forgiveness and guidance. There's no formula to get right; the priest will help guide you through it.

  • Scheduling: confession is by appointment with Fr. Rick or Fr. Peter — just reach out directly or through the parish office to find a time.
  • New to confession? that's completely alright to say up front. Many people preparing for Chrismation or returning to the Church after time away go to confession for the first time as adults.
  • Confidentiality: what's shared in confession is held in strict confidence.
